An open-water current reference in a wide, deep strait rather than a passage
feature. Flood sets 090 true, ebb 270 true.

Predicted peaks reach about 3.4 knots flooding and 3.7 knots ebbing on the
biggest tides, with a typical peak nearer 1.5 to 2 knots — worth working with on
a slow boat, rarely enough to block a passage. The ebb is both stronger and more
frequent, consistent with the strait's net seaward outflow. A summer sea breeze
reinforces the prevailing southwesterly and can reach 30 knots by late afternoon,
with calms common in early morning.

One timing note, computed from CHS predictions rather than published: through
summer, the strongest ebbs cluster from mid-to-late morning, tailing off after
early afternoon. That shifts the usual "afternoon westerly against a big ebb"
picture — the worst overlap is late morning into early afternoon, while the ebb
is still running hard and the sea breeze is filling in.

Source confidence: high — CHS Sailing Directions PAC201 (2024/05) and US Coast
Pilot 10 Ch. 7, both primary. Speed and timing statistics are derived from CHS
prediction data, not quoted figures. No source supports rips at Hein Bank; the
bank named for rips in heavy weather is Middle Bank. Neither authority documents
Pacific swell as a significant factor at the eastern end, so none is claimed here.
