Boundary Pass runs about 13 nm northeast from Turn Point on Stuart Island to the
Strait of Georgia, exiting between East Point on Saturna and the west end of
Patos. It is 1.5 to 5 nm wide, generally deep, and the international border runs
down the middle.

Both the US and Canadian sailing directions say plainly that the pass presents no
difficulty in clear weather — the caution they attach is to reefs in specific
places and to the rate and shifting direction of the streams, not to the channel
itself. Mid-channel is open water. The hazards cluster at the two ends and along
the edges: the Saturna–Patos gateway northeast, Turn Point southwest. Mid-passage,
commercial traffic is the real concern, not current. A Killer Whale Restriction
Area applies around East Point from 1 June to 30 November.

Two answers on slack, both true. CHS gives 10 to 12 minutes at the east-end
station; NOAA's predictions at Turn Point give a much wider usable low-current
window. Boundary Pass is forgiving to transit compared with a narrows like Dodd
or Porlier — it is not forgiving if the plan depends on genuinely slack water at
the east end.

Note on the station: PUG1717 bin 35 is the shallowest bin available but still
sits about 116 ft below the surface, so its speeds understate the surface stream.
That is consistent with Coast Pilot's 6-knot Turn Point ebb against the roughly
4.7-knot bin maximum — depth attenuation, not bad data. Use the bin-specific set
directions (flood 003, ebb 236); the NOAA station-info page's approximate flood of
040 disagrees with them.
